    Understanding Interviews

    What is an Interview?

    Definition & Purpose

    A structured conversation to assess mutual fit, skills, and readiness for a job role.

    The Employer's View

    Employers evaluate whether candidates align with company culture and job requirements.

    The Candidate's View

    You showcase your qualifications and assess whether the organization fits your career goals.

    Interview Formats

    Interviews may be in-person, by phone, video call, or panel — each requires the same preparation.

    Interviewing is a Skill

    🎯

    Practice

    • Improve with mock interviews
    • Build confidence through repetition
    • Self-reflection sharpens responses
    📋

    Preparation

    • Research reduces anxiety
    • Know the job description cold
    • Plan attire and logistics early
    💪

    Feedback

    • Use rubrics to self-evaluate
    • Peer review reveals blind spots
    • Each interview teaches you more

    The Interview Process

    Five phases — every successful interview follows this arc

    1

    Preparation Phase

    Research the employer, review the job description, practice responses, plan attire and route.

    2

    The Opening

    Greetings and introductions — first impressions set the entire tone of the conversation.

    3

    Core Discussion

    The main interview — questions are asked, detailed answers given, skills are demonstrated.

    4

    Closing Strong

    Ask your prepared questions, express continued interest, and leave on a confident note.

    5

    Follow-Up

    Send a thank-you note within 24 hours and reflect on the experience for future growth.

    Preparing for the Interview

    Preparation Tips

    🔍 Research the Employer

    Learn the company's mission, culture, and recent news to show genuine interest and ask informed questions.

    📋 Review the Job Description

    Study requirements carefully and align your specific skills and experiences to exactly what they need.

    🎤 Practice Common Questions

    Rehearsing out loud — not just in your head — dramatically improves confidence and response quality.

    👔 Plan Attire & Arrival

    Choose professional clothing the night before and plan to arrive 10-15 minutes early. No surprises.

    Researching the Employer

    Learn the company's purpose, goals, products, and services. Review their website, mission statement, and recent news.

    Familiarize yourself with their values and culture so you can align your responses naturally during the interview.

    Employers are genuinely impressed by candidates who reference specific company achievements in their answers.

    Body Language Matters

    Positive Signals

    Smile, maintain eye contact, use open gestures — project genuine enthusiasm throughout.

    Posture & Engagement

    Sit upright, lean slightly forward, nod to show active listening. Your body says you care.

    Fidgeting, checking your phone, crossing your arms, or slouching send the wrong message.

    A firm, confident handshake at the start and end sets a professional, memorable tone.

    Interview Techniques & Follow-Up

    The STAR Method

    A structured approach for answering behavioral interview questions

    S

    Situation

    Describe the specific situation you faced. Give enough context for the interviewer to understand.

    T

    Task

    Explain what you needed to accomplish — your specific role and responsibility in that situation.

    A

    Action

    Describe what YOU personally did. Use past tense — tell what you did, not what you would do.

    R

    Result

    Share the outcome. What happened? What did you accomplish? What did you learn from it?

    Instructor Resource Available STAR Interview Worksheet

    STAR in Action: Handling Stress

    Interviewer: "Tell me about a time you faced a stressful situation that demonstrated your coping ability."

    Situation + Task

    I had worked five 12-hour shifts in a row and was asked to stay when we were slammed with urgent exam requests.

    Action

    I agreed to stay and we worked as a team, prioritizing the most critical exams first and supporting each other.

    Result

    We turned a stressful situation into a manageable one. Patients were well cared for and pleased with our service — and I was recognized for my commitment.

    STAR in Action: Upset Customer

    Interviewer: "Tell me about a situation in which you had to deal with a very upset customer or co-worker."

    Situation + Task

    I was working in a nursing home when a patient's very upset family member told me the room hadn't been cleaned in two days.

    Action

    I reassured the family member it would be handled immediately, reported to my supervisor, and a housekeeper was assigned right away.

    Result

    The family was pleased with our quick response and felt genuinely heard. The patient was well cared for and the family expressed gratitude to our team.

    Questions to Ask the Interviewer

    Always come prepared with thoughtful questions — it demonstrates genuine interest and initiative.

    • What characteristics describe the person best suited for this job?
    • What advancement opportunities are available with your company?
    • What type of people tend to do well in this position — and who does not?
    • Are there any reservations about my fit for this position I could address?
    ★ Must Ask

    When will you be making a decision, and how will I be notified?

    During & Closing the Interview

    ☕

    While You're There

    • Maintain good posture throughout
    • Organize thoughts before answering
    • Refer to resume when appropriate
    • Express willingness to learn
    👍

    Wrapping Up

    • Ask your prepared questions
    • Thank the interviewer sincerely
    • Smile and shake hands confidently
    • Express continued interest
    🌟

    Leave an Impression

    • Restate your enthusiasm for the role
    • Exit with the same energy you entered
    • Be gracious to everyone you pass

    Follow-Up Etiquette

    1

    Send a personalized thank-you note or email within 24 hours — reference one specific thing discussed to make it memorable.

    2

    Reflect on the experience — note what went well and one thing you would handle differently next time.

    3

    If you have not heard back after one week, make a polite follow-up call or send a brief, courteous email.

    4

    Keep applying to other positions — never stop your job search while waiting on one opportunity.

    Before the Interview

    • Research the company — website, mission, recent news, and culture
    • Review the full job description and match your skills to it

    • Reflect on what went well and what you would do differently

    STAR Method Practice: Think It Through

    Open each question and discuss how you would frame a STAR answer as a class.

    Strategy: Choose a real weakness you have genuinely worked to improve. Use STAR to show the Situation that revealed it, your Task to address it, the Action steps you took, and the positive Result. Example: "I used to struggle with public speaking (S). I needed to lead team briefings (T). I joined a local speaking group and volunteered to present at staff meetings (A). Now I'm regularly asked to present to clients (R)."

    Strategy: Keep it under 2 minutes. Cover: (1) your current or most recent relevant experience, (2) a key skill or achievement that matches this job, (3) why you are excited about this specific role. Do NOT recite your entire life story — keep it professional and targeted to the position.

    Strategy: Connect your specific skills directly to what the job description calls for. Use evidence — a STAR-style example — to back up your claim. "Based on what I've read about this role, you need someone who [X]. In my previous position I [specific action and result]. That's exactly the kind of value I'd bring here." Confident, specific, relevant.

    Have students draft a 60-second answer to the first question on paper before you open it. Then compare their written answers to the strategy revealed — discuss what they got right and where they can tighten their responses.
