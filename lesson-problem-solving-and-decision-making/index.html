    Critical Thinking Quiz

    This short quiz was developed to test critical thinking skills. Try to answer each question before revealing the answer!

    Q1: How do you put a giraffe into a refrigerator?

    Open the refrigerator, put in the giraffe, and close the door.

    This tests whether you tend to do simple things in an overly complicated way.

    Q2: How do you put an elephant into a refrigerator?

    Open the refrigerator, take out the giraffe, put in the elephant, and close the door.

    This tests your ability to think through the repercussions of your previous actions.

    Q3: The Lion King is hosting an animal conference. All animals attend except one — which one?

    The elephant — it’s still in the refrigerator!

    This tests your memory.

    Q4: You must cross a river used by crocodiles, and you don’t have a boat. How do you do it?

    Jump in and swim across — all the crocodiles are at the animal conference!

    This tests whether you learn quickly from your mistakes.

    Problem-Solving Styles

    People try to solve problems in one of four main ways. These are known as problem-solving styles.

    You will probably use a variety of styles, but it’s a good idea to know which style you prefer.

    Complete the questionnaire to discover your dominant style, then we’ll explore what each one means.

    Professor

    Professor

    Seeks background info & theory. Values objectivity. Learns from past situations. Guard against: indecision from wanting too much information.

    🤝

    Diplomat

    Diplomat

    Seeks consensus by talking to people. Values opinions. Very persuasive. Guard against: missing timelines from gathering too many opinions.

    🔍

    Detective

    Detective

    Finds root causes with facts & observation. Follows consistent methods. Guard against: inflexibility and insensitivity to people.

    🏆

    Champion

    Champion

    Results-oriented, trusts instincts. Tries solutions hands-on. Great in a crisis. Guard against: impulsiveness and uncooperativeness.

    The Four-Step Process

    Watch: Decision-Making Strategies

    Four Steps to Better Decisions

    A logical method for solving problems and making decisions (Christenson et al., 2008)

    Step 1 Stop! Think about the problem
    Step 2 Options What are some choices?
    Step 3 Choose Pick the best one
    Step 4 Do It! Apply your decision

    The Four Steps in Detail

    Stop! Think About the Problem

    When presented with a problem, we sometimes react in an extreme manner — either by being impulsive or by procrastinating.

    • What might happen if we react impulsively?
    • What might happen if we procrastinate?
    • How could outcomes differ if we stopped first, then thought, then decided?

    What Are Some Options?

    Write a list of possible solutions — also called options or choices.

    • Brainstorm random solutions, even “outside the box” ones
    • After your list is full, eliminate choices that won’t work
    • Cross out options that are unethical, illegal, or harmful

    Choose One

    Look at the remaining options on your list.

    • If two choices remain, select the one that is most effective without causing negative effects
    • If only one viable option remains, it may be the best choice
    • Weigh short-term vs. long-term consequences

    Do It!

    Apply the remaining option to your situation.

    • Take action on your chosen solution
    • Observe the results
    • If the solution doesn’t work, go back to Step 2 and try another option

    Let’s Try It Together

    Scenario: Your kids need shirts for school, and with your limited budget, you look for a bargain on your favorite store’s website. You find a great sale on children’s shirts at 3 for $10! You rush to the store — but find the same shirts listed at $15 each, no sale sign in sight!

    What could you do?

    • Stop — Think about the problem
    • Options — Brainstorm possible solutions
    • Choose — Eliminate what won’t work
    • Do it — Apply your best option

    Lost at Sea — Expert Rankings

    You are adrift in the South Pacific after a yacht fire. Rank these 15 items for survival.

    Rank from 1 (most important) to 15 (least important):

    ☐ Sextant

    ☐ Small transistor radio

    ☐ Shaving mirror

    ☐ Shark repellent

    ☐ 5 gallons of water

    ☐ 20 sq. ft. opaque plastic

    ☐ Mosquito netting

    ☐ 1 case of rations

    ☐ Maps of the area

    ☐ Seat cushion (flotation)

    ☐ 2 gal. oil/gas mixture

    ☐ 1 qt. 160-proof rum

    ☐ 15 ft. nylon rope

    ☐ 2 boxes of chocolate bars

    ☐ Fishing kit

    1. 1. Shaving mirror — vital for signaling rescue
    2. 2. Oil/gas mixture — floats and can be ignited for signaling
    3. 3. 5 gallons of water
    4. 4. 1 case of rations
    5. 5. Opaque plastic — collect rainwater, shelter
    6. 6. Chocolate bars — reserve food
    7. 7. Fishing kit — no guarantee of catching fish
    8. 8. Nylon rope — secure equipment
    9. 9. Seat cushion — lifesaver if someone falls in
    10. 10. Shark repellent
    11. 11. Rum — 80% alcohol, antiseptic only; causes dehydration
    12. 12. Transistor radio — no transmitter
    13. 13. Maps — worthless without navigation tools
    14. 14. Mosquito netting — no mosquitoes at sea
    15. 15. Sextant — useless without tables and chronometer

    Lunar Survival — NASA Rankings

    Your ship crash-landed 200 miles from the mothership on the moon. Rank these 15 items.

    Rank from 1 (most important) to 15 (least important):

    ☐ Box of matches

    ☐ Food concentrate

    ☐ 50 ft. nylon rope

    ☐ Parachute silk

    ☐ Portable heating unit

    ☐ Two .45 caliber pistols

    ☐ Dehydrated milk

    ☐ Two 100-lb. oxygen tanks

    ☐ Stellar map

    ☐ Self-inflating life raft

    ☐ Magnetic compass

    ☐ 20 liters of water

    ☐ Signal flares

    ☐ First aid kit w/ injection needle

    ☐ Solar FM receiver-transmitter

    1. 1. Oxygen tanks — most pressing survival need
    2. 2. 20 liters of water — replacement for body fluid loss
    3. 3. Stellar map — navigation
    4. 4. Food concentrate — efficient energy supply
    5. 5. FM receiver-transmitter — communication with mothership
    6. 6. Nylon rope — climbing, tying injured together
    7. 7. First aid kit — aiding the injured
    8. 8. Parachute silk — sun protection
    9. 9. Life raft — CO2 bottle for self-propulsion
    10. 10. Signal flares — distress signal when mothership is visible
    11. 11. Pistols — possible self-propulsion
    12. 12. Dehydrated milk — similar to food concentrate
    13. 13. Heating unit — not needed on lit side of moon
    14. 14. Magnetic compass — no polarized magnetic field on moon
    15. 15. Matches — worthless, no oxygen on the moon
