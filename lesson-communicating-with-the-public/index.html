    Introduction

    Why Communication Matters

    What is Communication?

    Effective communication is the exchange of information, intention, and emotion.

    It goes beyond just speaking — it includes understanding, active listening, and reading nonverbal cues.

    🗣

    Communication in the Workplace

    Employer Priority

    Over 70% of employers prioritize strong communication skills in job candidates — sometimes more than a specific degree or major.

    Top Skill in Job Postings

    Communication consistently ranks as the #1 skill in job postings, yet many employers report a shortage of candidates with these abilities.

    Professional Growth

    Strong communication skills build self-confidence and help you achieve both personal and professional goals.

    Forms of Communication

    Written, verbal, nonverbal, digital — modern workplaces require all forms of communication mastery.

    Elements of Communication

    Encoding & Decoding

    The two sides of every message

    📤 Encoding (Sender)

    The process of converting thoughts, ideas, or information into a message using symbols — words, gestures, images, or sounds — that the receiver can understand.

    📥 Decoding (Receiver)

    The process by which a receiver interprets and makes sense of a message. Tone, body language, and personal experiences all influence interpretation.

    The Communication Cycle

    1

    Sender encodes — Person #1 formulates and sends a message

    2

    Receiver decodes — Person #2 receives and interprets the message

    3

    Receiver becomes sender — Person #2 encodes a response

    4

    Original sender decodes — Person #1 receives and interprets the response

    Discussion

    Why is this communication process called a cycle? What happens when the cycle breaks down?

    Hearing vs. Listening

    Hearing

    A passive, physical process. Sound waves enter your ears automatically — no effort required.

    Listening

    An active, intentional process. It requires effort, attention, and the desire to understand the speaker's message.

    • Listening is NOT a natural process — it requires effort
    • All listeners do NOT receive the same message
    • Personal experiences and biases shape how we interpret what we hear

    Listening Skills

    🎯

    Attention Skills

    Pay attention to the speaker's words and body language. Don't allow yourself to be distracted.

    🔍

    Clarifying Questions

    Ask questions about unclear statements, missing information, or anything that could be interpreted more than one way.

    💡

    Reflective Skills

    Reflect back to the speaker the message as you understood it. What emotions did you hear? Did you understand the concept?

    🚫

    Overcoming Obstacles

    Recognize distractions, personal emotions, opinions of the speaker, and other things on your mind that interfere with listening.

    Communication Styles

    Three Communication Styles

    Verbal Message

    The words you choose carry your meaning. Pay attention to:

    • Pronunciation — Pronounce words correctly
    • Grammar — Proper usage and correctness matters
    • Vocabulary — Watch for overuse of words
    • Slang — Stay away from popular phrases in professional settings
    • Swearing — Avoid at all costs in public communication

    Body Message

    Your body speaks even when your mouth is closed:

    • Facial Expressions — Ensure your expression matches the message you want to convey, not necessarily how you feel
    • Posture — Open, upright posture signals engagement and confidence
    • Eye Contact — Maintains connection and shows attentiveness
    • Gestures — Use intentional hand movements to reinforce your message

    Voice Message

    How you say it matters as much as what you say:

    • Pitch — The highness or lowness of your voice
    • Volume — Loudness or softness appropriate to the setting
    • Tone — Emphasis and inflection convey attitude
    • Rate — Speaking speed affects comprehension
    • Enunciation — Clarity and correctness of pronunciation

    Congruent Messages

    When your verbal message, body message, and voice message all align, you send a congruent message.

    Incongruent messages — where your words say one thing but your body or voice say another — create confusion and erode trust.

    Self-Advocacy

    What is Self-Advocacy?

    Self-advocacy is the ability to express your needs, make informed choices, and seek support when necessary.

    It means speaking up for yourself respectfully and clearly — in your personal life, education, and the workplace.

    💪

    Self-Advocacy in Action

    1

    Speaking up for yourself in conversations and meetings

    2

    Staying informed and knowing how to get information

    3

    Knowing your rights and responsibilities

    4

    Finding people who will support you

    5

    Solving problems and reaching out when you need help

    6

    Taking control of your own life = self-determination

    Why Self-Advocacy Matters

    Personal Confidence Builds belief in your own abilities
    Professional Advancement Enhances success in your career
    Interpersonal Communication Strengthens community relationships
    Holistic Empowerment Maintains healthy life balance

    Building Self-Advocacy Skills

    Key Components of Self-Advocacy ▼
    • Know Yourself — Understand your strengths, needs, and goals
    • Know Your Rights — Be aware of legal and workplace protections
    • Communicate Clearly — Express needs directly and respectfully
    • Set Goals & Make a Plan — Define what you want and how to get there
    How to Practice Self-Advocacy ▼
    • Identify Your Needs — Reflect on what you require in a situation
    • Gather Information — Research your options and resources
    • Practice Speaking Up — Rehearse what you want to say
    • Use "I" Statements — "I need..." or "I feel..." keeps the focus on you
    • Be Persistent but Respectful — Advocacy is a process, not a one-time event
    Overcoming Barriers to Self-Advocacy ▼
    • Fear of Rejection or Conflict — Start small; practice in low-stakes situations first
    • Lack of Knowledge — Research your rights and available resources
    • Lack of Confidence — Build confidence through preparation and practice

    Remember: Self-advocacy is a skill that improves with practice. Every time you speak up, it gets easier.

    Tips for Effective Self-Advocacy

    1

    Stay calm and collected — Manage your emotions before speaking

    2

    Be specific — Clearly state what you need and why

    3

    Use positive body language — Eye contact, open posture, steady voice

    4

    Keep records — Document conversations, agreements, and follow-ups

    5

    Be patient — Change takes time; persistence pays off
